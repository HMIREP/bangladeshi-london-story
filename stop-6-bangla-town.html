Stop 6 – Bangla Town

Rise and fall: the story of the Curry Mile and the shifting economic heart.

What this stop covers

This section of Brick Lane was officially designated "Banglatown" in the late 1990s as part of a branding exercise to promote the area as a cultural and culinary destination.

We discuss the transition from the hidden economy of **garment sweatshops** (where many first-generation Bangladeshis worked behind closed doors) to the highly visible **service economy** of the "Curry Mile." We also look at the decline: how a strip that once boasted around 60 restaurants now has fewer than half that number remaining.

Key facts

  • The street furniture—including the red and green lamp posts—was designed by Bengali artists to mirror the colors of the Bangladesh flag.
  • In the 1980s and 90s, the "curry house" became the primary symbol of Bangladeshi economic success in the UK, but it also became a target for racist "Paki-bashing" during the height of National Front activity.
  • Today, the high cost of rent and the changing tastes of a gentrified London are forcing many traditional family-run restaurants to close.

Questions to bring with you

  • Does turning a neighborhood into a "branded" tourist destination (like Banglatown) help preserve its culture, or does it turn it into a museum?
  • When a traditional industry like the curry trade begins to fade, what happens to the families who have relied on it for generations?
