Stop 6 – Bangla Town

Economic evolution: from the hidden labor of the garment trade to the visible branding of the "Curry Mile."

What this stop covers

In the late 1990s, this section of Brick Lane was officially designated as "Banglatown." This stop analyzes the shift from the "hidden economy" of garment sweatshops—where many first-generation migrants worked in subdivided houses—to the highly visible "service economy" of the restaurant trade.

We examine how the branding of an ethnic enclave as a tourist destination impacts the local community. While it provided economic visibility, it also coincided with the rise of gentrification, which has seen the number of traditional curry houses drop from over 60 to approximately 23 in recent years.

Key facts

  • The street's aesthetic markers—including the red and green lamp posts—were specifically designed to mirror the national flag of Bangladesh.
  • During the 1980s and 90s, the "curry house" was both a symbol of economic mobility and a primary target for racist provocations by far-right groups.
  • The economic transition of the 2010s saw the area's specialized garment workshops replaced by tech offices and high-end retail, fundamentally altering the local labor market.

Questions for reflection

  • Does the formal "branding" of a neighborhood like Banglatown protect its heritage or merely package it for outside consumption?
  • When a traditional family-run economy (like the restaurant trade) declines, what institutional or social structures are left to support the resident community?

Independent Inquiry

Research the 1991 Spitalfields City Challenge reports or the socio-economic analysis by Tower Hamlets Council regarding the decline of the Brick Lane restaurant trade in the 2010s.
