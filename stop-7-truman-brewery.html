Stop 7 – Truman Brewery

Gentrification in real-time: corporate power vs. community heritage.

What this stop covers

The Truman Brewery, once the largest brewery in the world, is now the frontline of a major battle over the future of Brick Lane. We stand here to discuss gentrification—the process where a poor urban area is changed by wealthier people moving in and improving housing, often displacing the original community.

The recent controversial planning application to build a massive shopping mall and office complex on the brewery site has sparked the "Save Brick Lane" campaign. Activists argue that this development will "smother" the local Bangladeshi culture and drive up rents until no local businesses can survive.

Key facts

  • Established in 1666, Truman's Black Eagle Brewery was a massive employer for centuries until it closed in 1989.
  • In the 1990s, the brewery became a hub for "creative industries," art shows, and fashion—a move that initially helped the area but also signaled the start of its rapid gentrification.
  • Over 7,000 objections were filed against the recent redevelopment plans, making it one of the most contested planning applications in East London history.

Questions to bring with you

  • Who owns the "soul" of a street? The people who have lived and fought there for 50 years, or the company that owns the land?
  • Can "regeneration" ever happen without displacing the people who made the area famous in the first place?
