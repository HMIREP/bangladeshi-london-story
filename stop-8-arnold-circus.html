Stop 8 – Arnold Circus

Utopia in brick: 130 years of social housing and the birth of a new East End.

What this stop covers

Our tour ends at Arnold Circus, the center of the Boundary Estate. This was the world's first large-scale social housing project, built by the London County Council (LCC) to replace the "Old Nichol," one of Victorian London's most notorious and dangerous slums.

Standing on the central mound—which is built from the literal rubble of the slums that were cleared away—we reflect on how housing policy has shaped the lives of migrant families. From the Victorian era to the 1970s housing actions, the story of the East End has always been a story of people fighting for a decent place to live.

Key facts

  • The Boundary Estate was opened in 1900 by the Prince of Wales. It replaced a slum where the infant mortality rate was twice that of the rest of London.
  • The design was revolutionary: wide streets, a laundry, a clubhouse, and apartments that allowed for light and air—luxuries the poor had never known.
  • Today, the estate remains a mix of social housing (housing many Bangladeshi families) and high-priced private apartments, showing how the history of social welfare and modern gentrification sit side-by-side.

Questions to bring with you

  • When we "clear a slum" to build something better, who is left behind? Did the people of the Old Nichol actually get to live in these new, beautiful buildings?
  • Looking back at our whole walk—from the hospital where I was born to this bandstand—what is the common thread that makes someone a "Londoner"?
