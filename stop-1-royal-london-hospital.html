Stop 1 – Royal London Hospital / Town Hall

Beginnings: birth, migration, and the institutional foundations of a borough.

What this stop covers

This opening stop connects my own birth at the Royal London Hospital in 1967 with my father's arrival as a merchant seaman in the 1940s. We utilise this site to explore the long trajectory of migrant patients, workers, and residents who have passed through these halls.

We also introduce the pivotal Quddus Ali case of the 1990s. We examine how a landscape once marked by racist violence and mobilisation transitioned into a borough led by Bangladeshi political representation, and analyse the resulting shifts in civic power.

Key facts

  • The Royal London Hospital was established in the 18th century as one of Britain's earliest charitable healthcare institutions.
  • Tower Hamlets currently maintains one of the highest proportions of Muslim and Bangladeshi residents of any local authority in England and Wales.
  • The original hospital structure now serves as the Tower Hamlets Town Hall, the administrative centre where critical decisions regarding housing and urban planning are made.

Questions for reflection

  • What are the psychological implications when a site of birth also becomes a focal point for political protest and community defence?
  • In the transition from a public hospital to a town hall, how has the nature of "power" shifted for the local community?

Independent Inquiry

For primary source records on the 1993 mobilisation, research the Tower Hamlets Local History Library & Archives regarding the Quddus Ali case.
