Here to Stay

A London Story of Bangladeshis

Eight Historical Stops

üìç 1. Royal London Hospital

We begin with my birth in 1967 and the 1993 Quddus Ali rally. This building frames vital questions of violence, healthcare, and political power in the East End.

üìç 2. East London Mosque

The institutional backbone of the borough. A site of spiritual sanctuary and community struggle since its founding in 1941.

üìç 3. Fieldgate Mansions

Where the Bengali Housing Action Group (BHAG) turned legal squatting into permanent homes, winning a landmark victory for housing rights in the 1970s.

üìç 4. Altab Ali Park

Commemorating the 1978 racist murder that sparked a 7,000-strong march to Downing Street and the defiant slogan "Here to stay, here to fight."

üìç 5. Brick Lane Mosque

A "palimpsest" of migration. Once a Huguenot chapel, then a Methodist church, then a synagogue, and since 1976, a mosque.

üìç 6. Banglatown

Walking through the heart of the "curry capital." We reflect on the rise and shift of the garment and restaurant trades along Brick Lane.

üìç 7. Truman Brewery

A live confrontation with gentrification. We discuss the "Save Brick Lane" campaign and the ongoing battle for community space in 2026.

üìç 8. Arnold Circus

The tour ends at the Boundary Estate. Here, 130 years of housing policy, from Victorian slum clearance to modern regeneration, can be read in the brickwork.

About the Seminar

Led by Hussain Ismail, this two-hour walking seminar traces the line from 1940s merchant seamen to modern political representation. Access to archival photos, audio clips, and further reading is provided via this portal.
