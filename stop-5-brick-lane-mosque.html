Stop 5 – Brick Lane Mosque

The Palimpsest: three centuries of migration written in brick and stone.

What this stop covers

At the corner of Brick Lane and Fournier Street stands a building that acts as a palimpsest—a historical document that has been overwritten multiple times while retaining traces of its past identities.

This single structure has transitioned from a Huguenot chapel (1743) to a Methodist church (1819), then to a Great Synagogue (1898), and finally to a Mosque (1976). Each shift mirrors a major wave of displacement and arrival in East London's history.

Key facts

  • The sundial on the south wall still bears the Latin inscription "Umbra Sumus" (We are shadows), carved by the Huguenots in 1743.
  • The building survived the 19th-century transition from Protestant to Jewish worship as the area became the heart of the "Jewish East End" following pogroms in Russia.
  • Across all phases, the surrounding "weaver's houses" provided the domestic infrastructure for the garment trade—moving from silk weaving to tailoring and later to leather work.

Questions for reflection

  • Does the fact that the same building has served three different faiths suggest a continuity of human experience that transcends religious dogma?
  • What does the survival of the 1743 sundial tell us about the persistence of memory in a neighborhood that is constantly being "rebranded"?

Independent Inquiry

Examine the Survey of London (Volume 27, Spitalfields) for architectural diagrams of 59 Brick Lane, and research the Huguenot Society records regarding La Neuve Eglise.
