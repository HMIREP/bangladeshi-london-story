Stop 5 – Brick Lane Mosque

The Palimpsest: three centuries of migration written in brick and stone.

What this stop covers

At the corner of Brick Lane and Fournier Street stands a building that is a palimpsest—a document that has been erased and overwritten multiple times, yet still shows traces of its original writing.

This single structure has served as a place of worship for three different migrant groups, each arriving in London fleeing persecution or seeking economic survival, and each leaving their mark on the building's architecture and the surrounding streets.

Key facts

  • 1743: Built as La Neuve Eglise, a Protestant chapel for Huguenots fleeing religious persecution in France. Look up at the sundial on the south wall; it still bears the French inscription "Umbra Sumus" (We are shadows).
  • 1898: Converted into the Machzikei Hadass (Spitalfields Great Synagogue) for Jewish immigrants escaping pogroms in Russia and Eastern Europe.
  • 1976: Converted into the Brick Lane Jamme Masjid to serve the growing Bangladeshi community.
  • Across all three waves, the surrounding houses were used as workshops for the garment trade—silk weaving for the Huguenots, tailoring for the Jews, and leather/garment work for the Bangladeshis.

Questions to bring with you

  • Why do you think the same building was able to serve three different religions over 280 years? What does this tell us about the "nature" of migration in the East End?
  • The sundial says "We are shadows." Do you think migrant communities are shadows in London, or are they the light that makes the city's culture visible?
