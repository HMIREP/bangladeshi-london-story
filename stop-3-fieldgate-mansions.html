Stop 3 – Fieldgate Mansions

Housing as resistance: the legacy of the Bengali Housing Action Group and the fight for a home.

What this stop covers

This stop explores the critical history of housing struggles in the 1970s. For many newly arrived Bangladeshi families, the choice was between unsafe, overcrowded private rentals or literal homelessness.

We discuss the Bengali Housing Action Group (BHAG), which organised mass squats in blocks like Fieldgate Mansions. This was more than a search for shelter; it was a sophisticated political movement that utilised legal "Section 6" notices to claim the community's right to remain in the heart of London.

Key facts

  • Fieldgate Mansions were originally Victorian tenement blocks built for the Jewish community at the turn of the 20th century, illustrating the overlapping histories of East End migration.
  • In 1976, BHAG was formed as a grassroots response to the local council's failure to provide adequate housing for the Bengali community.
  • The movement eventually won a landmark victory, forcing the authorities to grant permanent tenancies to hundreds of squatting families and sparking the large-scale "regeneration" of these blocks into social housing.

Questions for reflection

  • In the context of the 1970s, was the act of "squatting" a criminal endeavour or a necessary form of civic engagement?
  • How did BHAG’s success change the institutional perception of the Bangladeshi community—moving them from the role of passive migrants to that of active, organised citizens?

Independent Inquiry

For primary source materials on the housing movement, research the Bishopsgate Institute archives regarding the Bengali Housing Action Group (BHAG) and the 1970s photography of Nick Hedges or Raju Vaidyanathan.
