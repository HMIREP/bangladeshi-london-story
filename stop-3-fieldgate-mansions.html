Stop 3 – Fieldgate Mansions

Squatting for a home: how the Bengali Housing Action Group claimed space.

What this stop covers

This stop explores the vital history of housing struggles in the 1970s. For many newly arrived Bangladeshi families, the choice was between unsafe, overcrowded private rentals or homelessness.

We discuss the Bengali Housing Action Group (BHAG), which organized mass squats in blocks like Fieldgate Mansions. This wasn't just about finding a roof; it was a political statement that the community was "here to stay" and would not be ignored by local housing authorities.

Key facts

  • Fieldgate Mansions were originally Victorian tenement blocks built for the Jewish community at the turn of the 20th century.
  • In 1976, BHAG was formed as a grassroots response to the council's failure to house Bengali families.
  • At its peak, hundreds of families were squatting "legally" (using Section 6 notices) in the East End, leading to a massive change in local housing policy and the eventual "regeneration" of these blocks into permanent social housing.

Questions to bring with you

  • Is it "illegal" to occupy a building that has been left empty for years while families are homeless on the street?
  • How did the success of BHAG change the way the local council viewed the Bangladeshi community—as passive victims or active citizens?
