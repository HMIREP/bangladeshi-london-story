Stop 2 – East London Mosque

The institutional backbone: a history of spiritual sanctuary and community struggle since 1941.

What this stop covers

This stop examines the East London Mosque as the institutional heart of the Bangladeshi and wider Muslim community in Tower Hamlets. We explore how this site was built through grassroots donations and political organising rather than state-led initiatives.

We connect the mosque’s development to the broader narrative of migration in Whitechapel, reflecting on the fact that Tower Hamlets now contains the highest proportion of Muslim and Bangladeshi residents of any local authority in the United Kingdom.

Key facts

  • The mosque's origins date back to the early 20th century; the current landmark complex was opened in stages beginning in the 1980s.
  • According to the 2021 Census, approximately 39.9% of Tower Hamlets residents identify as Muslim, representing a significant demographic anchor for the borough.
  • Roughly one-third of the local population identifies as Bangladeshi, establishing this area as the cultural and political "blood centre" for the diaspora.

Questions for reflection

  • How does the existence of a central mosque that serves as a school, welfare hub, and political forum change the civic character of a London borough?
  • In what ways does the physical presence of this institution challenge historical narratives of migrant "transience" in the East End?

Independent Inquiry

Research the East London Mosque Archives or the Museum of London regarding the 1910 London Mosque Fund and the early maritime organising of the Jamiat-ul-Muslimin.
