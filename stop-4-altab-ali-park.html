Stop 4 – Altab Ali Park

Resistance and remembrance: the murder that galvanized a movement.

What this stop covers

This park is named after Altab Ali, a 25-year-old Bangladeshi garment worker who was murdered in a racist attack on May 4, 1978, as he walked home from work.

His death was a turning point. Ten days later, 7,000 people—largely from the Bengali community—marched behind his coffin from here to Downing Street. It was the birth of the slogan "Here to Stay, Here to Fight," signaling that the community would no longer accept being victims, but would actively defend their right to exist in London.

Key facts

  • The park sits on the site of St. Mary Matfelon, the original 14th-century "White Chapel" that gave the area its name.
  • In 1998, the park was officially renamed Altab Ali Park to commemorate his life and the anti-racist struggle.
  • The park features a Shaheed Minar (Martyr's Monument), a replica of the monument in Dhaka, Bangladesh, symbolizing the community's cultural and political roots.

Questions to bring with you

  • Why is it significant that this park—once the site of the church that named the area—is now named after a migrant worker murdered in a hate crime?
  • How did the slogan "Here to stay, here to fight" change the trajectory of the Bangladeshi community from being seen as "migrants" to becoming "citizens"?
