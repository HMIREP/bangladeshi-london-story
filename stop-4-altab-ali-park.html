Stop 4 – Altab Ali Park

"Here to Stay, Here to Fight": The 1978 turning point in community resistance and civil rights.

What this stop covers

This site, formerly known as St Mary's Churchyard, was renamed in 1998 to commemorate Altab Ali, a 25-year-old Bangladeshi garment worker murdered in a racist attack nearby on May 4, 1978.

His death acted as a catalyst for a massive community mobilization. Ten days after the murder, 7,000 people marched behind his coffin to Downing Street, signaling a definitive shift in the community's posture: from vulnerable migrants to organized citizens demanding safety and political representation.

Key facts

  • The park sits on the site of the 14th-century "White Chapel" (St Mary Matfelon) that gave the district its name.
  • The Altab Ali Arch, designed by David Petersen and installed in 2002, incorporates Bengali and European motifs to symbolize cultural synthesis and community resilience.
  • The park also houses a replica of the Shaheed Minar (Martyr's Monument), which connects the local community to the 1952 Language Movement in Bangladesh, reinforcing the transnational identity of the East End.

Questions for reflection

  • How does renaming a historical landmark after a victim of racial violence transform the "ownership" of urban space?
  • In what ways did the 1978 mobilization transition the Bangladeshi community from being the *subjects* of history to the *authors* of their own civic rights?

Independent Inquiry

Research the Altab Ali Foundation archives and the 1970s documentary photography of Paul Trevor, which captures the immediate aftermath of the 1978 march and the birth of the "Here to Stay" movement.
